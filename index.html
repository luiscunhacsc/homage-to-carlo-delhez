About this Document

This interactive page serves as both a tribute to Dr. Carlo Delhez and a structured resource designed to facilitate the creation of a Wikipedia entry. It documents his vital work in keeping the ZX81 platform alive through high-performance emulators and robust development tools, even during the era when the ZX Spectrum dominated the 8-bit landscape.

Biography & Context

In the history of home computing, the Sinclair ZX81 holds a unique place. Released in 1981, its stark monochrome graphics and membrane keyboard were quickly overshadowed by its colorful successor, the ZX Spectrum. However, for many, the ZX81 was the foundational gateway to programming.

Dr. Carlo Delhez recognized the enduring educational and technical value of this machine. Rather than abandoning the platform as the market moved on, Delhez dedicated significant effort to ensuring the ZX81 environment could survive and thrive on newer hardware. His work bridged the gap between the original 8-bit microcomputers and the emerging 16-bit PC-compatible and Motorola 68000 (Sinclair QL) architectures.

Emulation Masterpieces

One of Dr. Delhez's most remarkable technical achievements was his development of ZX81 emulators. Emulation in the late 1980s and early 1990s was exceptionally difficult due to the low clock speeds of host machines. Delhez managed to write emulation software that ran at 100% or more of the original machine's speed on a humble 10 MHz 80286 processor. He also extended this capability to the Sinclair QL.

Software & Development Tools

Beyond hardware preservation, Delhez enriched the ZX81 software ecosystem. His two most prominent contributions were Coral BASIC and a highly robust Z80 Assembler.

📖 Coral BASIC

An advanced BASIC dialect tailored for the ZX81 environment. It provided extended commands and optimized execution routines, allowing developers to write more complex and faster programs than the standard Sinclair ROM permitted, pushing the boundaries of the 1K-16K machine.

⚙ The Forgiving Assembler

Learning Machine Code (Z80) natively was notoriously difficult; a single misplaced byte would crash the machine, losing hours of unsaved work. Delhez's Assembler environment running via emulation featured state-saving and easy crash recovery.

Delhez Z80 Assembler Environment

Experience the memory: When learning Z80, a bad jump (`JP 0x0000` to the wrong place) meant a total lockup. In Delhez's environment, recovery was just keystrokes away—like reversing time in a video game.

Legacy & Community Impact

We strongly believe in keeping the memory of pioneers alive, especially within communities where their specific, highly technical contributions are deeply understood and recognized.

For a generation of programmers, Dr. Carlo Delhez was not just a developer; he was an enabler. By creating tools that forgave mistakes—allowing users to reverse a crash as if "reversing a few seconds before a character died in a game"—he lowered the barrier to entry for low-level programming.

His emulators ensured that when the physical hardware inevitably degraded, the software, the logic, and the culture of the ZX81 lived on flawlessly in the PC era.
